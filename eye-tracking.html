Eyeing translation behaviors

This eye-tracking research focuses on human translators and their behaviors, which can be instrumental to improve translation education and user experience of computer-assisted translation tools.

PROJECT OVERVIEW

PROJECT BACKGROUND

Background & Problem

The majority of translation studies traditionally focused on texts, linguistics and cultures, while less attention was directed to human translators and their work process. We believe that human-centered research will significantly benefit the studies of translation, as argued by Carl et al. (2011):

"An empirically-grounded cognitive model of translation processes can not only be instrumental for the improvement of translation education, but also provide a basis for the development of translation assistance tools and for more successful interaction between human translators and the technology."

Objectives

This research utilized eye tracking equipment to:

  • Identify a generalized cognitive model of human translators and subtasks during the entire translation process.
  • Analyze different levels of cognitive workload among distinct subtasks.
Heat map of a single participant

PROCESS

Define target

Identify experiment variables by literature review

Certain factors affecting gaze activities mentioned in previous researches, including time constraints, text complexity, etc., were evaluated and selected with regard to the objective.



Include crucial translation subtasks

By comparing real translation process of a translation-major student (scope of study) with previous literature, it was found that the subtask of consulting external source was rarely considered in previous empirical studies of translation activities. Considering its evident presence in the translation process, it was retained in this research, together with source text (ST) and target text (TT).

Design experiment settings

Design interface

Reflecting on the feedback of translation students, the interface was designed with primarily two considerations:

  • Tools required while working (source text, text editor and a dictionary) with reference to current commercial computer-aided translation tools
  • The precision of data grouping for analysis


Control variables

The subjects were required to translate two pieces of real texts of varied complexity in counterbalance order, and the whole process and gaze data were recorded. Further, known variables that might affect gaze activities, such as screen luminance and difference in color contrast, were controlled as much as possible.

OUTCOME

RESEARCH FINDINGS

By combining several measures of cognitive workload indicators, variations among the proposed subtasks were investigated of their cognitive intensity with quantitative analysis.


The findings can mainly be briefed in following aspects:

  • Distributional pattern of attention. Analyzed patterns of attention by dwell time in area of interests to find different levels of cognitive efforts required in respective subtasks.
  • Cognitive workload. Analyzed fixation duration with pupil size to investigate the cognitive workload among different subtasks. Discovered positive relationship between key events and fixation durations.
  • Identify new subtasks during analysis. Dictionary lookup activities were further divided into searching (with typing events) and browsing (w/o typing), showing different levels of cognitive workload.
  • Working styles of translators. Examined cross interest area saccades and the duration of their following fixations. Most attentional shifts took place between ST and TT as expected, however, no different patterns of dictionary lookup activities prompted by needs for comprehension or production were discovered, contradicting with previous literature.

LOOKING FORWARD

I definitely enjoy every moment working on this project from the scratch. I received precious guide and acquired new knowledge and skills. Due to limited time-frame, I think some aspects could be refined and further expanded as follows to generate practical benefits to current tools and environment for human translators:

  • Analyze with other source of data, such as key logging, for a more comprehensive understanding
  • Qualitative research for better interface design and text evaluation
  • Further insight into problem-solving behaviors on different linguistic levels and its effect on eye movement and cognitive intensity
  • What inspirations can be drawn for improving translation service and tools
